📊 Scoring System
ATS Core Term
Against The Spread. Instead of just picking who wins, you're betting on whether a team wins (or loses) by more or fewer points than the posted spread. A team can lose the game but still "cover" ATS if they lose by less than the spread.
Example: Chiefs are -7. You bet ATS. They win 24-20 (+4). They did NOT cover — you lose, even though they won.
Spread Core Term
The point handicap set by the sportsbook to level the playing field. The favorite gives points (-) and the underdog gets points (+). You're betting on the margin of victory, not the winner.
Example: Eagles -6.5 vs Cowboys. Eagles must win by 7+ to cover. Cowboys cover if they win OR lose by 6 or fewer.
Moneyline Core Term
A straight-up bet on who wins the game — no spread involved. Favorites have negative odds (you risk more to win less), underdogs have positive odds (you risk less to win more).
Example: Celtics -160. Bet $160 to win $100. Lakers +140. Bet $100 to win $140.
Over / Under (O/U) Core Term
A bet on the total combined score of both teams. The sportsbook sets a number; you bet whether the actual total will be Over or Under that number. Also called "the total."
Example: Total is 47.5. Final score is 28-24 = 52 total. The Over hits.
Cover Core Term
When a team beats the spread. If a team is favored by -6 and wins by 10, they "covered." If the dog is +6 and loses by 3, the dog "covered."
💡 In this dashboard, ATS W = number of times a team covered the spread this season.
-110 Odds Odds
The standard "vig" (commission) on most spread and total bets. You risk $110 to win $100. This is how sportsbooks make money — even on a 50/50 bet, you need to win 52.4% just to break even.
💡 Breakeven on -110 odds = 52.4% win rate. That's why this dashboard highlights teams over 55%.
Vig / Juice Odds
The sportsbook's built-in commission on every bet. Standard vig is -110 on both sides, meaning the book keeps ~4.5% regardless of outcome. Shopping for the best line reduces vig.
Example: Both sides at -110 means the book profits ~$10 per $220 wagered.
Line Movement Advanced
When the spread or total shifts from its opening number. Movement toward a side usually signals sharp (professional) money. If you bet early and the line moves in your favor, you likely have value.
Example: You bet Chiefs -6 early. Line moves to -8. Sharp money agrees with you.
Sharp vs. Public Advanced
Sharps are professional bettors who move lines. The public (recreational bettors) tend to bet favorites and popular teams. Many edges come from "fading the public" — taking the other side of heavy public action.
💡 When 80%+ of bets are on one side but the line barely moves or moves away, that's sharp action on the other side.
Push Core Term
When the final score lands exactly on the spread or total. Your original bet is refunded — no win, no loss. Sportsbooks use .5 half-points on spreads to prevent pushes.
Example: Spread is -3. Team wins by exactly 3. Push — money back.
ROI Tracking
Return on Investment. Net profit divided by total amount wagered, shown as a percentage. A +5% ROI is excellent in sports betting. The industry average for recreational bettors is around -5%.
Example: Bet $1,000 total, net +$80 profit = +8% ROI.
Parlay Bet Type
Combining 2+ bets into one ticket — all legs must win. The odds multiply, so payouts are bigger, but one loss sinks the whole ticket. Sportsbooks love parlays because the house edge compounds with each leg.
💡 Parlays are fun but -EV long term. Use sparingly and stick to 2-3 legs max.
ATS Record NFL / CFB
A team's wins and losses when betting the spread. This is different from their straight-up record. A team can be 10-6 in games but only 7-9 ATS if they regularly win by less than expected.
💡 In this dashboard: ATS W-L shows season ATS, Home ATS and Away ATS show splits.
Home ATS / Away ATS NFL / CFB
A team's ATS record split by home and away games. Some teams are great covers at home but bad on the road, or vice versa. Matching the right side to the right venue is a key edge.
Example: A team is 8-2 ATS at home but 3-7 ATS on the road — only back them at home.
SP+ Rating CFB
A college football advanced efficiency metric created by ESPN. Combines offensive and defensive efficiency, adjusted for opponent strength and pace. Higher SP+ Offense = more efficient scoring. Higher SP+ Defense = fewer points allowed relative to expectation.
💡 SP+ is one of the best predictors of future performance in CFB — better than win-loss record alone.
PPG / PPG Against NFL / CFB
Points Per Game scored (PPG) and Points Per Game allowed (PPG Against). Used in this dashboard to project game totals in the Matchup Analyzer by averaging both teams' offensive and defensive rates.
Example: Team A scores 28 PPG, Team B allows 24 PPG → Team A projects ~26 pts.
Off Rank / Def Rank NFL
A team's ranking in offensive and defensive efficiency across the entire league. #1 Off Rank = most efficient offense. #1 Def Rank = stingiest defense. Lower number is better.
💡 Big gaps between Off Rank and Def Rank help spot mismatches — a top offense vs. a weak defense often means an Over lean.
Divisional Game NFL / CFB
A game between two teams in the same division. Historically these games are tighter, with big favorites covering at lower rates. Teams know each other well, and pride/rivalry factors level the field.
💡 In the Matchup Analyzer, flagging a divisional game automatically warns you to fade large favorites.
Rest Edge NFL / CFB
The advantage a team gains from having more days off before the game. In the NFL, a team off a bye week (10+ days) vs. a team on a short week is a meaningful edge — rested teams cover ~54%.
Example: Team A has 10 days rest, Team B played 4 days ago. Team A has a +6-day rest edge.
Look-Ahead Spot NFL / CFB
When a team has a huge game coming up next week and may be emotionally/physically focused ahead. They often underperform vs. the current week's opponent. A common fade spot.
Example: Team plays a rival in a "trap game" the week before a College Football Playoff game.
Offensive Rating (OffRtg) NBA
Points scored per 100 possessions. The league average is typically ~115. Higher = more efficient offense. Used to project scoring and spot mismatches independent of pace.
Example: Team with OffRtg 125 vs. team with DefRtg 108 = massive mismatch, lean Over or back the offense.
Defensive Rating (DefRtg) NBA
Points allowed per 100 possessions. Lower = better defense. Paired with OffRtg to get Net Rating (OffRtg - DefRtg), the best single indicator of overall team quality.
💡 Net Rating = OffRtg minus DefRtg. A team with +8 Net Rating is an elite team regardless of record.
Pace NBA / CBB
Estimated number of possessions per 40 minutes (CBB) or 48 minutes (NBA). High pace = more possessions = more scoring opportunities = lean Over. Slow pace = fewer possessions = lean Under.
💡 In the Matchup Analyzer, when both teams avg above 101 possessions (NBA), the dashboard flags an Over lean automatically.
Back-to-Back (B2B) NBA
When a team plays on consecutive nights. B2B teams — especially on the road — are one of the most reliable fade spots in the NBA. They cover as favorites at only ~44%.
💡 Always check the schedule before betting NBA. A home team vs. a B2B road favorite is a strong lean.
AdjOE / AdjDE CBB
KenPom's Adjusted Offensive Efficiency and Adjusted Defensive Efficiency. Points scored/allowed per 100 possessions, adjusted for opponent strength. The gold standard for evaluating college basketball teams.
Example: AdjOE 120 = scores 120 pts per 100 possessions vs. avg D-1 defense. Elite is 115+.
AdjEM CBB
KenPom's Adjusted Efficiency Margin = AdjOE minus AdjDE. The single best number for ranking CBB teams. Top teams are typically +20 to +35. Used in the Matchup Analyzer to flag large mismatches.
💡 When one team's AdjEM is 12+ points better, the spread often underestimates the true talent gap.
Tempo CBB
KenPom's adjusted possessions per 40 minutes for a college team. Teams above 70 possessions are fast-paced; below 65 is slow. Matchup Analyzer uses the average of both teams' tempo to project game pace and flag Over/Under leans.
Example: Both teams average 72 possessions = high pace = lean Over.
Neutral Site CBB
A game played at a venue where neither team is the "home" team — common in tournaments, bowls, and early-season events. Home court advantage (~3.5 pts in CBB) is removed. ATS records become the primary handicapping factor.
💡 Always flag neutral site games in the Matchup Analyzer — it adjusts the analysis accordingly.
Puck Line NHL
The NHL's version of a point spread — but it's always fixed at 1.5 goals. The favorite gives -1.5 (must win by 2+) and the underdog gets +1.5 (can lose by 1 and still cover). Unlike football spreads, the odds change to compensate — laying -1.5 with a strong team gives you better payout odds than the moneyline.
Example: Oilers -1.5 at +120. Bet $100, win $120 — but they must win by 2+ goals.
💡 Home underdogs on the +1.5 puck line cover ~56% of the time — one of the most consistent NHL edges.
PL W / PL L NHL
Puck Line Wins and Losses — a team's record when betting with the 1.5-goal spread. A team can win the game (moneyline win) but still lose the puck line if they win by exactly 1 goal. Both records matter for different bet types.
Example: Team goes to OT and wins 3-2. Moneyline: WIN. Puck Line (-1.5): LOSS.
Power Play % (PP%) NHL
The percentage of power play opportunities a team converts into goals. League average is around 19-21%. Teams above 23% have an elite power play that can shift game totals and outcomes meaningfully.
💡 In the Matchup Analyzer, a home team with PP% above 23% gets flagged as a scoring multiplier edge — especially in high-penalty games.
Penalty Kill % (PK%) NHL
The percentage of opposing power plays a team successfully kills (prevents from scoring). League average is ~78-80%. A team with 85%+ PK% neutralizes even elite power plays.
Example: Team A has PP% of 25%, but Team B has PK% of 86% — the power play edge is neutralized.
Goals For / Goals Against (GF/GA) NHL
Total goals scored (GF) and total goals allowed (GA) for the season. Divided by games played to get goals per game averages, which the Matchup Analyzer uses to project totals.
💡 When both teams average under 2.8 GA/game, the Under is a strong lean — two elite defenses rarely produce high-scoring games.
OTL (Overtime Loss) NHL
A loss that goes to overtime or a shootout. In the NHL standings, a regulation win = 2 pts, an OTL = 1 pt (both teams get a point). Important for standings context — teams can be "close" in points despite very different records.
Example: Team A is 35-14-6. Their "points" = (35×2)+(6×1) = 76 standings pts.
Starting Goalie NHL
The single biggest variable in NHL betting. If the starting goalie changes — especially to a backup — the entire moneyline value shifts dramatically. Always check warm-up lineups before placing an NHL bet.
💡 A backup goalie starting for the favorite can flip a -160 moneyline to essentially a pick'em. This is the #1 reason to shop lines late in NHL.
Over/Under (NHL) NHL
Just like other sports — bet the total combined goals. NHL totals typically range from 5.5 to 7.0. Key factors: goalie matchup, special teams efficiency, pace of both teams, and whether the game is early in a back-to-back.
💡 When both starting goalies are elite and both teams have strong PK%, the Under is usually the value play.
Run Line MLB
Baseball's version of a point spread — always fixed at 1.5 runs. The favorite gives -1.5 (must win by 2+) and the underdog gets +1.5. Like the puck line, the odds adjust to compensate for the fixed spread.
Example: Dodgers -1.5 at +125. Must win by 2+ to cash, but pays better than the moneyline.
💡 Home underdogs on the +1.5 run line are one of the most profitable long-term bets in MLB.
ATS (MLB) MLB
In baseball context, ATS typically refers to the run line record (1.5). A team's ability to win by 2+ (or lose by 1 or less as a dog) over the course of a season. Tracked the same way as spread ATS in football.
💡 Teams with strong bullpens tend to have better ATS records because late-inning leads get protected and extended.
RS/G & RA/G MLB
Runs Scored per Game (RS/G) and Runs Allowed per Game (RA/G). The core stats for projecting game totals. The Matchup Analyzer averages the offensive and defensive rates from both teams to project a raw total.
Example: Team A scores 4.8 RS/G, Team B allows 4.2 RA/G → Team A projects ~4.5 runs.
Team ERA MLB
Earned Run Average for the entire pitching staff (starters + bullpen combined). Lower is better. The season-long ERA gives context, but the starting pitcher's individual ERA matters most for a specific game bet.
💡 Always enter today's starting pitcher's ERA in the Matchup Analyzer for the most accurate projection.
Batting Average (.AVG) MLB
The team's collective batting average — hits divided by at-bats. Used as a proxy for offensive contact quality. Teams hitting .260+ are typically above-average offenses; below .240 is weak.
Example: A team batting .272 against a pitcher with a 2.80 ERA is a tough matchup for the offense.
Wind (Ballpark Factor) MLB
Wind direction and speed at the ballpark is one of the most reliable MLB totals edges. Wind blowing OUT (toward the outfield) 15+ mph = lean Over 3-4 pts. Wind blowing IN = lean Under 3-4 pts. Use Weather.com and check the specific stadium direction.
💡 Wrigley Field (Cubs) is the most wind-sensitive park in baseball. Always check wind before betting a Cubs total.
Bullpen MLB
The relief pitchers who take over after the starter exits. A rested, fresh bullpen can lock down a game; a tired one (4+ relievers used in last 3 days) is a liability, especially for teams favored to protect leads late.
💡 Check bullpen usage before betting a team to win late. An overworked bullpen is one of the best fade spots in MLB.
Home / Away ATS MLB
A team's run line record split by home vs. away games. In baseball, home field matters less than in football, but teams playing in their home park know the quirks (wall distances, foul territory, wind patterns) better than visitors.
Example: A team is 12-6 ATS at home but 5-13 away — only back them in their park.
Confidence Tiers How It Works
Every pick is scored 0–100. That number IS the model's estimated cover/win probability — a 65 means 65% chance of covering. Scores map to tiers: 🔥 Elite (68+), ⚡ Strong (62–67), ✅ Good (57–61), 📊 Lean (<57). All tiers are logged for performance tracking to measure calibration over time.
💡 Breakeven for -110 juice is 52.4%. Any pick rated Good or above has a real mathematical edge.
F1 — ATS Record Split (32%) Factor
The single heaviest factor. Uses the team's home ATS record when they're at home, away ATS when they're on the road. For CBB (neutral site), the overall season ATS record is used instead. All ATS signals are Bayesian-shrunk — small sample sizes are pulled toward 50% to prevent hot streaks from dominating early in a season.
Example: A team is 8-2 ATS at home (80%) with n=10 games. Shrunk to ~65% — still strong, but tempered.
F2 — Implied Probability Edge (20%) Factor
Compares the team's shrunk ATS win rate to the no-vig implied probability from the market spread. A positive edge means the team covers more often than the market expects. Uses the same Bayesian-shrunk rate as F1 — raw percentages would double-count hot small-sample records.
Example: Market implies 52% cover probability. Team's shrunk ATS rate is 62%. That's a +10 point edge — meaningful signal.
F3 — Rest Edge (12%) Factor
Measures the days-of-rest difference between both teams. A team with 10+ days off vs. a team on a short week has a meaningful physical and preparation edge. In the NBA, this factor is replaced by F8b when a back-to-back situation is detected — they can't both fire on the same game.
Example: Team A has 4 days rest, Team B played last night. +3 day edge for Team A → F3 boosts their score.
F5 — Season ATS Overall (15%) Factor
The team's full-season ATS record regardless of home/away. Acts as a second ATS signal that smooths out home/away variance with a larger sample size. Skipped entirely for CBB — it would double-count F1, which already uses the overall record for neutral-site games.
💡 F1 and F5 together give a home-split signal and a full-season signal. When they agree, the combined weight approaches 47% of the final score.
F6 — Efficiency Differential (20%) Factor
Measures how much better one team is than the other on both sides of the ball. NBA uses Net Rating (OffRtg − DefRtg). CBB uses KenPom AdjEM (AdjOE − AdjDE). MLB uses runs scored/allowed per game from the season totals (with a 162-game fallback at season start so Opening Day still works). A big efficiency gap means the spread likely underestimates the true talent gap.
Example: Team A Net Rating +9.2, Team B Net Rating −1.4. F6 gap = 10.6 → strong lean toward Team A.
F7 — Injury Signal (10%) Factor
Penalizes the picked team when key players are listed as out, and rewards them when the opposing team has significant injuries. The signal is based on the count and impact of active injury reports pulled from ESPN's injury data. A team's pick score drops when their own stars are out and rises when their opponent is shorthanded.
💡 This factor fires automatically — injuries are fetched live. No manual input needed.
F8a — Home Dog Angle (6%) Factor
A situational boost applied when a home team is an underdog and we're picking them to cover. Historically, home underdogs cover at ~53–54% in NFL, CFB, and NBA — the crowd and comfort factor is underpriced by the market. Disabled for CBB (neutral site games remove the home advantage entirely).
Example: Chiefs are -7 road favorites. We pick the home dog. F8a fires, adding ~6% weight to that side.
F8b — Back-to-Back Road Fade (9%) Factor
NBA-only. Fires when a team is playing their second game in two nights on the road as a favorite. This is one of the most reliable fade spots in sports — B2B road favorites cover at only ~44%. When F8b fires, F3 (rest edge) is suppressed to avoid counting the same fatigue signal twice.
Example: Lakers played last night in Denver. Tonight they're in Portland as -4 favorites. F8b fires → strong lean to Portland.
F9 — ML Underdog Floor (12%) Factor
If the moneyline model rates the underdog with 60%+ confidence, and ATS records are close (within 7%), the spread pick is overridden to the ML side. Rationale: if an underdog wins outright, they always cover the spread. ML confidence acts as a floor for spread picks when the outright upset looks likely.
💡 F9 only fires when the underdog is rated highly. It's a relatively rare override but prevents the model from picking a spread side while the ML model is screaming the other way.
Bayesian Shrinkage How It Works
A statistical technique applied to all ATS records before they enter the model. Small sample sizes are shrunk toward 50% (coin flip) because early-season records are unreliable. As the season progresses and sample sizes grow, the shrinkage fades. At 20+ games, the full ATS rate is used.
Example: 3-1 ATS (75%) in 4 games → shrunk to 56%. Same 75% after 25 games → trusted at ~73%. The model doesn't overreact to a hot start.
Confidence Score = Cover Probability How It Works
The confidence ring (0–100) directly represents the model's estimated cover or win probability — no extremizer applied. A score of 63 means the model estimates a 63% chance of covering the spread. This makes calibration trackable: over time, picks rated 63 should win roughly 63% of the time. Tier boundaries: Elite ≥68%, Strong ≥62%, Good ≥57%, Lean <57%.
Example: ATS history 69%, efficiency edge, rest advantage → weighted blend = 0.65 → displayed as 65 (Strong tier, 65% cover probability).
